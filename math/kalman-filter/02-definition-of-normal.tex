\section{Definition of Normal Distribution}

The \define{normal distribution} with mean $\mu$ and variance $\var$ is
defined as:

\begin{nedqn}
  \nnormal
& \defeq &
  \nnormaleq
  \\
  %
\shortintertext{And the \define{standard normal} is simply:}
  %
  \snormal
& \defeq &
  \snormaleq
\end{nedqn}

We ought to show this alleged probability distribution integrates to 1.
Let's do a change of variables $x \mapsto \parens{\sqrt{2}\sigma y +
\mu}$:

\begin{nedqn}
  \int_\reals
    \nnormaleq
& = &
  \sqrttwo\sigma
  %
  \int_\reals
    \nnormalc
    %
    \normalexpf{
      \Bparens{
        \parens{\sqrt{2}\sigma y + \mu}
        -
        \mu
      }^2
    }{2\var}
    %
    \dy
  \\
& = &
  \invsqrtpi
  \int_\reals
    \gaussianexp[y]
    \dy
  \\
& = &
  \invsqrtpi
  \sqrtpi
  \nedcomment{Gaussian integral}
  \\
& = &
  1
\end{nedqn}

Notice that factor of $\sqrttwo$ that got introduced when we did the
change of variables. That's because $\sqrttwo \dy = \dx$.

Anyway, this shows that $\nnormal$ is a proper probability density
function.
